Part One

Who He
Is

"People used to say I should be a lawyer because I argued a lot. After hearing it a hundred times — I thought, that's actually not a bad idea."

Yoel Zehaie is a Dallas/Fort Worth attorney practicing family law, criminal law, and civil litigation. His path to law started the way many of the best careers do — with a personality trait that kept showing up. People told him he argued too much. He heard it enough times that he decided to get paid for it.

In high school he joined the mock trial team — competing against other schools as a courtroom advocate before he was old enough to vote. He loved it immediately. The pressure. The strategy. The stakes. He never looked back.

"The high stakes cases are the ones that excite me most. Somebody depending on me — that gets me going."

Today Yoel runs Call Your Well Now — a DFW law practice built on two principles: never give up, and genuinely care about the client. He answers his own calls. He works with clients when they are down. He fights for outcomes that lesser attorneys would have settled or abandoned.

Part Two

What He
Believes

"We genuinely care about the client. That's what separates us."

Yoel's practice is built on a simple but increasingly rare foundation: he picks up the phone himself. No AI auto-attendant. No call screener. When you call Call Your Well Now, Yoel wants to be the one who answers — because he believes knowing every case, from the first conversation, is what makes the difference between a lawyer who shows up and a lawyer who wins.

His two non-negotiables as an attorney: never give up until the outcome is right, and if a client is struggling financially after already paying thousands, find a way to keep working with them. These are not policies. They are how he was built.

"The biggest mistake people make is waiting too late to hire a lawyer. By the time they call, the damage is already done."

He has seen it play out in family law, criminal cases, and civil litigation alike. Representing yourself in an emotionally charged case — custody, divorce, criminal charges — means you cannot see things objectively. You are attached. The law does not care about attachment. Yoel does. And he keeps that attachment from destroying you in court.

Part Three

The Case
That Stays
With Him

"This guy just had awful luck. And we are going to fix it."

One case has stayed with Yoel longer than most — a custody battle he has been fighting for over a year and a half. A father whose daughter is nearly 12. A mother who has made it her mission to keep them apart. And a courtroom record derailed by a previous attorney who simply did not show up to a hearing.

Because of that missed appearance, the father was placed on supervised visitation — meaning he could not see his daughter outside of a facility. Not because of anything he did. Because his lawyer was not there.

"False accusations. A lawyer who didn't show up. Two more lawyers who dropped the ball. This man just kept getting failed by the people who were supposed to protect him. That's why I took the case."

Yoel has spent a year and a half rebuilding the case from the ground up — gathering evidence, fighting false accusations, and working toward a hearing that he believes will restore the father's unsupervised visitation rights and reunite him with his daughter. He is emotionally invested. He says so openly. That investment, he believes, is exactly what the case requires.

Part Four

How to
Find the
Right Lawyer

"Don't just hire anybody. You don't want to pay tens of thousands and get screwed over."

Yoel is direct about something most attorneys won't say out loud: the legal industry is full of people who will take your money and underdeliver. He has seen the wreckage — clients who came to him after being failed by big firm names, previous attorneys who missed hearings, and lawyers who never should have taken the case in the first place.

His advice for anyone vetting an attorney is practical and personal: find someone a trusted friend actually knows. Not a firm with a big name and a billboard. Not a Google ad. A recommendation from someone who has experienced the work firsthand.

"Ask around. If you have a mutual friend who knows the lawyer — that's worth more than any review or reputation."

And if you are facing a criminal situation: do not say anything. Not to police. Not to anyone. Hire a lawyer first and let the lawyer handle it from there. Law enforcement is trained in interrogation. One slip is all it takes. Yoel has seen what happens when people think they can talk their way out. They cannot.
